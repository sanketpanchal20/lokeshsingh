Bottom line on comment types

Ranked by total volume across both projects: color/style (50) and text/grammar (46) are the two largest categories by far, followed by spacing/alignment (35) and cuts/zoom (27). Structure and audio/SFX are smaller and roughly tied (19 each). Frame glitches are the smallest by volume (8), but a frame glitch reaching review at all is unprofessional. A self review pass before uploading is how you catch these yourself instead of a client or a reviewer catching them for you.

So the highest leverage fix is the same one twice: a self review pass before uploading would catch most of the color and text notes, since those are the categories that repeat rather than the ones that need new skill.

A note that took three drafts to land

This is the clearest single example of a small thing slipping through more than once.

V1"Daniel's frame looks very pale, can you check what's wrong here?"
V3"Daniel is still pale here... needs to be fixed throughout the video"
V5"[Annotation] color not fixed"

Same root issue, flagged three separate times. When a note says "fix this throughout," it's worth checking every instance in the timeline before reuploading, not just the one that got pointed out.

Consistency across the runtime

One general pattern worth naming: effort visibly drops in the second half of a video, sometimes the last third. It's fine to simplify once a deadline is close, fewer animations, lighter graphics. But whatever ships in that back half still needs to hit the same bar as the front half. Fewer, good animations is the goal. Fewer, rushed ones isn't.

Accountability

This matters as much as the editing, maybe more. It's about how you work with the rest of the team.

41%
of your Slack messages contained a question
8+
times you flagged yourself as asking too much

A large share of those questions were reasonable. Some were avoidable, the kind with an obvious answer that just cost time on both sides while you waited for a reply. A few examples:

"so, should I send a video with all the changes done except the AI Brolls or should I send it on Monday including the AI Brolls?"
"there bro? Should I go with my understanding or should I upload it as it is on the frame to be reviewed by you?"
"can I add 2 liners in the first 30 seconds?"

Each of these was a call you had enough context to make yourself. Note the reasoning, make the decision, and get it reviewed after, not before.

The standard we're asking for: identify the problem, make a decision, act on it, then get it reviewed. If the decision was wrong, you'll get a note and fix it, that's normal. What we want to avoid is waiting for someone else to spot the problem before anything happens on your end.

On the flip side, the ownership moments were real, and worth continuing: owning mistakes directly when they happened, pushing back with reasoning when a note didn't apply, explaining that a cut you couldn't fully fix was baked into the source footage, and adjusting your workflow once something was explained rather than just going along with it without understanding why.

3

Final Words

Overall, you're doing good. The editing and design instincts are strong, and the improvement between the two projects, especially on language, is real and worth being proud of. The main thing to carry into the next project is tightening up attention to detail so notes don't repeat across drafts, and shifting communication toward making a call yourself, then getting it reviewed, instead of asking first. Keep the momentum, keep the ownership when things go wrong, and focus on communication style going forward.
